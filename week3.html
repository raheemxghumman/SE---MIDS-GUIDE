Software Requirements

Definition

A complete description of what the software system will do without describing how it will do it. Software requirements are a complete specification of the desired external behavior of the software system to be built.

IEEE Definition

A condition or capability that must be met or possessed by a system to satisfy a contract, standard, specification, or other formally imposed document. (IEEE Std 729)

Software requirements represent the external behavior of the system and may be expressed as:

  • Abstract statements of services and/or constraints
  • Detailed mathematical functions for precise specifications

Sources of Requirements

  • Stakeholders: People affected in some way by the system (users, managers, domain experts)
  • Documents: Existing documentation and specifications
  • Existing System: Current system being used
  • Domain/Business Area: Industry-specific knowledge and practices
Examples
  • The system shall maintain records of all payments made to employees on accounts of salaries, bonuses, travel/daily allowances, medical allowances, etc.
  • The system shall interface with the central computer to send daily sales and inventory data from every retail store.
  • The system shall maintain records of all library materials including books, serials, newspapers and magazines, video and audio tapes, reports, collections of transparencies, CD-ROMs, DVDs, etc.

Functional Requirements

What are Functional Requirements?

Statements describing what the system does - the functionality and services the system should provide, reactions to particular inputs, and how the system should behave in particular situations.

Key Characteristics

  • Describe the functionality of the system
  • Specify services the system should provide
  • Define reactions to particular inputs
  • Capture sequencing and parallelism
  • Document abnormal behavior as exception handling
  • Should be complete and consistent
Customers and developers usually focus all their attention on functional requirements. Make sure you can distinguish between functional and non-functional requirements!
Functional Requirements Examples
  • The software automatically validates customers against the ABC Contact Management System
  • The Sales system should allow users to record customer sales
  • Only Managerial level employees have the right to view revenue data
  • The software system should be integrated with banking API
  • The background color for all windows will be blue with hexadecimal RGB value of 0x0000FF

Non-Functional Requirements

What are Non-Functional Requirements?

Requirements that relate to the system as a whole rather than individual features. They include constraints on timing, performance, reliability, security, maintainability, accuracy, the development process, and standards.

Critical Importance

Non-functional requirements are often more critical than individual functional requirements. Failure to meet a non-functional requirement may make the whole system unusable (e.g., if an aircraft system doesn't meet reliability requirements, it won't be certified as 'safe').

Why NFRs Arise

  • User needs
  • Budget constraints
  • Organizational policies
  • Need for interoperability with other software/hardware systems
  • External factors (safety regulations, privacy legislation)

Key Characteristics

  • Capture the emergent behavior of the system
  • Relate to the system as a whole
  • Must be built into the framework of the software product
  • Often more critical than functional requirements

Types of Non-Functional Requirements

1. Product Requirements

Requirements which specify that the delivered product must behave in a particular way (e.g., execution speed, reliability).

Subcategories:

  • Efficiency Requirements:
    • Performance requirements (processed transactions/second, response time)
    • Space requirements (memory, storage)
  • Usability Requirements: Training time, help frames
  • Reliability Requirements: Mean time to failure, probability of unavailability
  • Portability Requirements: Target platform compatibility
Product Requirements Examples
  • The system shall allow one hundred thousand hits per minute on the website
  • The system shall not have downtime of more than one second for continuous execution of one thousand hours

2. Organizational Requirements

Requirements that are a consequence of organizational policies and procedures (e.g., process standards used, implementation requirements).

Subcategories:

  • Standards Requirements: Coding standards, documentation standards
  • Implementation Requirements: Programming language, development tools
  • Delivery Requirements: Delivery schedule, deliverables format
Organizational Requirements Examples
  • The system development process and deliverable documents shall conform to a specific standard
  • Any development work sub-contracted by the development organization shall be carried out in accordance with Capability Maturity Model

3. External Requirements

Requirements that arise from factors external to the system and its development process (e.g., interoperability requirements, legislative requirements).

Subcategories:

  • Legislative Requirements: Compliance with laws and regulations
  • Privacy Requirements: Data protection and confidentiality
  • Safety Requirements: Safety-critical system constraints
External Requirements Examples
  • The system shall not disclose any personal information about library members to other members except system administrators
  • The system shall comply with local and national laws regarding the use of software tools

Metrics for Specifying NFRs

Non-functional requirements should be written quantitatively whenever possible:

PropertyMeasure
SpeedProcessed transactions/second, User/event response time, Screen refresh time
SizeMbytes, Number of ROM chips
Ease of useTraining time, Number of help frames
ReliabilityMean time to failure, Probability of unavailability, Rate of failure occurrence
RobustnessTime to restart after failure, Percentage of events causing failure
PortabilityPercentage of target dependent statements, Number of target systems

Observations on NFRs

  • NFRs can be written to reflect general goals (ease of use, recovery from failure) but goals are open to misinterpretation
  • Objective verification is difficult for general goals
  • Distinction between functional and non-functional is not always clear
  • Should be written quantitatively when possible, though some goals have no quantitative measures (e.g., maintainability)
  • Goals can be useful to designers as they indicate customer priorities
  • Chances of conflicts within NFRs are high as information comes from different stakeholders
  • Negotiation among stakeholders is necessary to resolve conflicts
  • Should be highlighted in requirements document for architectural design
Non-Functional Requirements Examples
  • Users must change the initially assigned login password immediately after the first successful login. The initial password shall never be reused.
  • Employees are never allowed to update their salary information. Such attempts should be reported to the security administrator.
  • Every unsuccessful attempt by a user to access an item of data shall be recorded on an audit trail.
  • A website should be capable of handling 20 million users without affecting its performance.
  • The software should be portable so moving from one OS to another does not create problems.

Functional vs Non-Functional Requirements

Functional Requirements

  • What it is: Verb (actions)
  • Requirement: Mandatory
  • Captured in: Use cases
  • End-result: Product features
  • Focus: User requirement
  • Documentation: What the product does
  • Testing: Functional testing (System, Integration, End-to-End, API)
  • Execution: Before non-functional testing

Non-Functional Requirements

  • What it is: Attributes (qualities)
  • Requirement: Non-mandatory
  • Captured as: Quality attributes
  • End-result: Product properties
  • Focus: User expectations
  • Documentation: How the product works
  • Testing: Non-functional testing (Performance, Stress, Usability, Security)
  • Execution: After functional testing

Key Differences Summary

Functional RequirementsNon-Functional Requirements
Help understand the functions of the systemHelp understand the performance of the system
Explain characteristics a system is expected to haveExplain the way the product should work (how it behaves)
Identify what the system must or must not doIdentify how the system should do it
System will perform even if NFRs are not metSystem will not work with NFRs alone
Ensures system meets user requirementsEnsures product meets user expectations
Essential to system operationsMay be desirable but not always essential
Straightforward to define and agree onHarder to define and agree on
Easy to captureHard to capture
Remember: A functional requirement defines a system or its component, whereas a non-functional requirement defines the performance attribute of a software system. FR is a VERB (what to do), NFR is an ATTRIBUTE (how well to do it).

Requirements Engineering Processes

The processes used for requirements engineering vary widely depending on the application domain, the people involved, and the organization developing the requirements. However, there are generic activities common to all processes.

Goal of RE

The goal of requirements engineering is to help create and maintain a system requirements document.

Four Main RE Activities

1. Elicitation 2. Analysis 3. Validation 4. Management
  1. Requirements Elicitation: What services do the end-users require of the system?
  2. Requirements Analysis: How do we classify, prioritize, and negotiate requirements?
  3. Requirements Validation: Does the proposed system do what the users require?
  4. Requirements Management: How do we manage the (sometimes inevitable) changes to the requirements document?

Feasibility Studies

Purpose

A feasibility study decides whether or not the proposed system is worthwhile. It's a short, focused study that checks if the system is viable from technical, economic, and organizational perspectives.

What Feasibility Study Checks

  • If the system contributes to organizational objectives
  • If the system can be engineered using current technology and within budget
  • If the system can be integrated with other systems that are used
  • Is there a simpler way of doing this (buy-in software and customize)

Feasibility Study Implementation

Based on information assessment (what is required), information collection, and report writing. Key questions for people in the organization:

  • What if the system wasn't implemented?
  • What are current process problems?
  • How will the proposed system help?
  • What will be the integration problems?
  • Is new technology needed? What skills?
  • What facilities must be supported by the proposed system?

Requirements Elicitation and Analysis

Sometimes called requirements elicitation or requirements discovery. Involves technical staff working with customers to find out about the application domain, the services that the system should provide, and the system's operational constraints.

Stakeholders

May involve end-users, managers, engineers involved in maintenance, domain experts, etc. These are called stakeholders.

ATM System Stakeholders
  • Bank customers
  • Representatives of other banks
  • Bank managers
  • Counter staff
  • Database administrators
  • Security managers
  • Marketing department
  • Hardware and software maintenance engineers
  • Banking regulators

Problems of Requirements Analysis

  • Stakeholders don't know what they really want
  • Stakeholders express requirements in their own terms
  • Different stakeholders may have conflicting requirements:
    • Staff want ease of use, management wants highest security
    • Patients want to change appointments easily, management wants to reduce costs
  • Requirements change during the analysis process - new stakeholders may emerge and the business environment changes

Requirements Discovery

The process of gathering information about the proposed and existing systems and refining the user and system requirements from this information.

Sources of information include:

  • Documentation
  • System stakeholders
  • Specifications of similar systems

Viewpoints

Viewpoints are a way of structuring the requirements to represent the perspectives of different stakeholders. This multi-perspective analysis is important as there is no single correct way to analyze system requirements.

Viewpoint Identification Using:

  • Providers and receivers of system services
  • Systems that interact directly with the system being specified
  • Regulations and standards
  • Sources of business and non-functional requirements
  • Engineers who have to develop and maintain the system
  • Marketing and other business viewpoints

Elicitation Techniques

1. Interviewing

The RE team puts questions to stakeholders about the system that they use and the system to be developed.

Closed Interviews

  • Pre-defined set of questions
  • Structured approach
  • Easier to analyze

Open Interviews

  • No pre-defined agenda
  • Range of issues explored
  • More flexible discussion
Benefits
  • Interactive discussion with stakeholders
  • Immediate follow-up to ensure understanding
  • Encourages participation and builds relationships
Drawbacks
  • Time required to plan and conduct interviews
  • Commitment required from all participants
  • Training may be required to conduct effective interviews

Best Practice: Interviewers should be open-minded, willing to listen to stakeholders, and should not have pre-conceived ideas.

2. Prototyping

Used to identify missing or unspecified requirements. Frequent demos are given to the client by creating prototypes so they can get an idea of how the product will look.

Benefits
  • Gives a visual representation of the product
  • Stakeholders can provide feedback early
Drawbacks
  • If the system is highly complex, prototyping may become time-consuming
  • Stakeholders may focus on design specifications rather than requirements

3. Survey/Questionnaire

A set of questions is given to stakeholders to quantify their thoughts. After collecting responses, data is analyzed to identify areas of stakeholder interest.

Open-Ended Questions

  • Respondent provides answers in their own words
  • More detailed information
  • Time-consuming to interpret

Close-Ended Questions

  • Predefined set of answers
  • Multiple choice or ranking
  • Easier to analyze
Benefits
  • Easy to get data from a large audience
  • Less time required for participants to respond
  • More accurate information compared to interviews
Drawbacks
  • Not all stakeholders might participate
  • Questions may not be clear to all participants
  • Open-ended questions require more analysis
  • Follow-up surveys might be required

4. Ethnography/Observation

A social scientist spends considerable time observing and analyzing how people actually work. People do not have to explain or articulate their work.

Key insights:

  • Social and organizational factors of importance may be observed
  • Ethnographic studies have shown that work is usually richer and more complex than suggested by simple system models
Benefits
  • Get information in a single session rather than one-to-one interviews
  • Active discussion creates a healthy environment
  • Learn from others' experiences
Drawbacks
  • Difficult to gather the group on the same date and time
  • Limited participant interaction in online methods
  • Skilled moderator required to manage focus group discussions

Requirements Management

Requirements management is the process of managing changes to requirements throughout the project lifecycle. It involves:

  • Requirements change management: Handling inevitable changes to requirements
  • Requirements traceability: Tracking requirements from origin through implementation
  • Requirements versioning: Maintaining different versions of requirements
  • Requirements prioritization: Determining which requirements are most important
Why Requirements Change
  • New stakeholders emerge
  • Business environment changes
  • Better understanding of the problem
  • Technology changes
  • Organizational changes
Be prepared to explain all four RE processes (elicitation, analysis, validation, management) and at least three elicitation techniques with their benefits and drawbacks.
